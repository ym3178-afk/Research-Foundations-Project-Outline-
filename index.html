01RESEARCH QUESTIONS

Research Questions

What disappears when the city is reduced to an estimated time?

The project asks how platform interfaces, urban infrastructure, and labor regulation shape what counts as an efficient delivery.

L

Labor

How do delivery platforms redistribute waiting, risk, and unpaid time onto couriers?

D

Design

How does the interface turn a complex urban journey into a smooth route and a single ETA?

P

Policy

What evidence could inform future rules for pay, data access, transparency, and worker protections?

E

Ethics

Can computational mapping expose algorithmic power without reproducing worker surveillance?

Relationship to the Precedent Study

Forensic Architecture informs the project’s use of reconstruction and spatial evidence. This work is a methodological fork: it shifts from disputed acts of state violence to the everyday operations of algorithmic labor, without treating the two as equivalent.

PrecedentThis Project
Space as evidence→Routes and waiting as evidence
Event reconstruction→Delivery reconstruction
Official narrative→Platform efficiency narrative
Invisible violence→Invisible pressure, risk, and labor
Maps, images, models→Website, timeline, drawing, object

This project does not equate platform labor with border violence; it reinterprets spatial reconstruction as a method for examining everyday algorithmic control.

Data + Site

Morningside Heights and West Harlem as a street-level case study

The project uses a citywide policy and labor context, then focuses field observation on Broadway, Amsterdam Avenue, restaurants, campus thresholds, and residential destinations.

01Street movement

Broadway and Amsterdam organize north–south delivery circulation.

02Campus thresholds

Entrances, gates, and interior paths create access time not visible in route distance.

03Mixed destinations

Restaurants, dormitories, offices, and residential buildings produce different forms of waiting.

Evidence Protocol

What will be observed, recorded, and ethically excluded

Field material will document restaurant waiting, street conflict, and building access. Worker routes or screenshots will be used only with consent, anonymization, and clear control over publication.

13EXHIBITION COMPONENTS

Beyond the Screen

The project is presented as a printed spatial argument, a material installation, and an optional interactive screen.

The exhibition translates algorithmic delivery from a screen-based abstraction into a sectional drawing, a bodily field of delay, and an interactive research interface.

01

Printed Vertical Drawing

A Delivery in Section

A tall investigative section reconstructing one delivery across restaurant, street, bicycle lane, building entrance, elevator, hallway, and apartment threshold. The drawing reveals that delay is an accumulation of spatial transitions.

Format: vertical printed drawingProposed size: 36 × 72 inLocation: Avery 100 trackMedia: map, section, timeline, evidence annotations
02

Material–Spatial Gesture

The Delay Curtain

A walk-through field of transparent strips, printed evidence, clips, and calibrated metal weights. Strip length represents delay duration; material and annotation indicate friction category; weight translates accumulated labor burden into physical resistance.

Form: walk-through installationMaterials: clear strips, thread, clips, steel weightsData mapping: length = duration; weight = burdenExperience: visitors move through the delays
03

Optional Second Screen

Optional Interactive Second Screen

An optional screen may show an interactive map, visualization, demo, or video during the final review. It supports the printed drawing and material–spatial gesture but is not a required submission component.

Format: web visualization or short videoInteraction: route, timeline, evidence categoriesPurpose: comparison and explanationStatus: optional final-review component
